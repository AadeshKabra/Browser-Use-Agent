Potential Directions:

Direction A: Double down on the research depth. Most cold email tools do shallow personalization — they plug in {first_name} and {company} into a template. Your browser automation agent can do something they can't: actually read the person's recent work. Imagine the agent reads their last 3 LinkedIn posts, skims a blog they wrote, notices they just launched a product feature — and the email references that specific thing. That's the difference between a cold email that gets deleted and one that gets a reply. The technical challenge becomes: how do you summarize diverse web content into a 2-sentence personalization hook that feels genuine, not creepy?

Direction B: Flip it from outreach to lead discovery. Instead of "here's a person, email them," the interesting problem becomes "here's my resume and what I'm looking for — find the right 20 people I should be talking to." The agent crawls job boards, company career pages, LinkedIn, identifies roles that match your profile, finds who the hiring manager likely is, and ranks targets by fit. The email drafting is the easy part — the targeting is where the intelligence lives.

Direction C: Make it a full recruiting intelligence tool. Combine A and B — discover leads, research them deeply, generate outreach, track responses, and learn from what works. The feedback loop is what makes it non-trivial: which email styles get replies? Which companies respond to which angles? Over time the system gets better at both targeting and messaging. This is closer to a real product than a script.

Direction D: Pivot away from cold email entirely. Maybe the browser automation agent is the real asset, and cold email is just one use case that undersells it. You could frame it as a general "research agent" — give it any information-gathering task on the web, and it handles it. More of an infrastructure play, less of a product.
